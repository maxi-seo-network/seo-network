Security Comparison: Model One vs Model T

Security is the primary reason to own a hardware wallet, and both devices deliver robust protection. Understanding the nuanced differences helps clarify which approach fits your threat model.

Chip Architecture

Neither the Model One nor Model T includes a Secure Element chip—a dedicated security processor found in newer devices like the Trezor Safe series. Instead, both rely on open-source firmware running on general-purpose ARM processors:

  • Model One: ARM Cortex-M3 @ 120 MHz—capable and proven, but slower than newer devices
  • Model T: ARM Cortex-M4 @ 168 MHz—approximately 40% faster, enabling smoother touchscreen operation

The absence of a Secure Element means physical access to either device could potentially enable sophisticated attacks. However, the open-source nature allows the community to verify the security model, and real-world successful attacks remain extremely rare.

Screen Security

The display plays a crucial role in transaction verification:

  • Model One: The 0.96" monochrome OLED screen (128x64 pixels) requires scrolling for longer addresses but remains perfectly readable. Physical buttons mean you must press to confirm, eliminating accidental approvals.
  • Model T: The 1.54" color LCD touchscreen (240x240 pixels) shows full transaction details at once. Touch confirmation is intuitive but some users prefer the deliberate physical buttons of the Model One.

Passphrase Protection

Both devices support passphrase wallets—an additional password beyond your seed phrase that creates hidden wallets. However, there's a critical difference:

  • Model One: Passphrase entry uses the two physical buttons to select characters—a slower process but resistant to keyboard loggers on your computer.
  • Model T: Full touchscreen keyboard allows easier passphrase entry directly on the device, making it more practical for longer passphrases.

On-device passphrase entry is crucial for security because it prevents your passphrase from being exposed to malware on your connected computer.

MicroSD Card Security

The Model T's MicroSD slot enables plausible deniability—you can configure the device to require an SD card for certain operations. This means you could have a "decoy" wallet visible without the SD card and a hidden wallet accessible only with it. The Model One lacks this feature entirely.

User Experience: Setup and Daily Use

Initial Setup

Model One: Setup requires connecting to Trezor Suite, updating firmware, and writing down your 12- or 24-word seed. The two-button interface makes navigation deliberate but slow. PIN entry uses arrow buttons to select digits—the process takes longer but feels secure.

Model T: Setup is more streamlined thanks to the touchscreen. You can choose between 12-, 20-, or 24-word seeds, or use Multi-share Backup (Shamir) to split your seed into multiple shares. PIN entry on the touchscreen resembles a smartphone keypad—faster but requires careful attention.

Daily Operations

For routine tasks like checking balances, receiving funds, or making transactions:

  • Model One: Simple tasks are straightforward. Complex operations requiring address verification involve scrolling through the small screen. Physical confirmation (holding both buttons) prevents accidental clicks.
  • Model T: Touchscreen navigation is faster and more intuitive. Full addresses display without scrolling. Transaction details are clearer on the larger color screen.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is better: Trezor One or Model T?

The Trezor Model T offers more features including a color touchscreen, MicroSD card slot, and Multi-share Backup support. However, the Trezor One provides excellent basic security at a lower price point. Choose Model T if you want advanced features and a touchscreen interface; choose Trezor One if you prefer a simple, affordable hardware wallet with proven security.

Are Trezor One and Model T still supported in 2026?

Yes, both devices remain fully supported. Trezor has committed to software updates until at least 2031 and critical security fixes until at least 2036 for both Model One and Model T. However, both models are discontinued for new purchases, replaced by the newer Trezor Safe series.

Does Trezor One support the same coins as Model T?

Both devices support Bitcoin, Ethereum, and thousands of other cryptocurrencies through Trezor Suite. The Model T may have better compatibility with some newer coins due to its more powerful processor. Check Trezor's official supported coins page for the most current compatibility information.

Can I use Trezor One or Model T without a computer?

Both devices require connection to a computer or mobile device to view your portfolio and initiate transactions. The Trezor Suite app is used to manage your assets. However, all private keys remain on the device and transactions must be confirmed physically on the hardware wallet itself.

What happens if I lose my Trezor device?

If you lose your Trezor device, you can recover your funds using your recovery seed (backup words). You'll need to purchase a new Trezor or use a compatible wallet and enter your recovery seed in the correct order. This is why it's critical to store your recovery seed securely and separately from your device.
