Fees Explained: What You'll Actually Pay

Understanding all the costs involved in buying Bitcoin helps you make informed decisions and avoid surprises. Here's a complete breakdown of the fees you might encounter.

Trading Fees

Trading fees are charged when you execute buy or sell orders. These are typically calculated as a percentage of your transaction:

  • Binance: 0.1% for both makers and takers (you can reduce this with BNB tokens or higher trading volumes)
  • Bybit: 0.1% maker/taker
  • Coinbase: Approximately 0.5% + a spread of about 0.5%

Deposit Fees

How much you pay to move EUR onto an exchange:

  • SEPA transfers: Usually free on Binance and Bybit; Coinbase may charge a small fee depending on your bank
  • Card deposits: Typically 1.5% - 3.99% depending on the exchange
  • MB Way: Varies by platform

Withdrawal Fees

When you move Bitcoin off an exchange to a personal wallet, small network fees apply. These are paid to Bitcoin miners for processing transactions, not to the exchange. Withdrawal fees are typically minimal (a few euros worth of BTC) but can spike during periods of network congestion.

Spread Costs

The spread is the difference between the buy price and sell price of Bitcoin. This represents an implicit cost, especially visible on platforms like Coinbase where spreads can be 0.5% or higher. While not a explicit fee, it's built into the price you pay.

Pro tip: For the lowest overall costs, use Binance with SEPA deposits and limit orders. This combination minimizes every type of fee.

Security Tips: Protect Your Bitcoin Investment

Bitcoin ownership comes with personal responsibility. Unlike traditional bank accounts, there's no customer service number to call if something goes wrong. Follow these security best practices to keep your investment safe.

Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Always enable 2FA on your exchange account. Use an authenticator app like Google Authenticator or Authy rather than SMS-based verification. SMS 2FA is vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks where hackers transfer your phone number to their device.

Use a Hardware Wallet for Large Holdings

Exchanges are targets for hackers. While reputable platforms invest heavily in security, keeping large amounts of Bitcoin on an exchange is risky. Hardware wallets store your private keys offline, making them immune to online attacks.

The Ledger hardware wallet is one of the most trusted options in the market. Ledger devices range from €50-250 and support Bitcoin along with dozens of other cryptocurrencies. Think of it as insurance for your digital assets.

Avoid Phishing Scams

Scammers constantly target cryptocurrency users through:

  • Fake emails mimicking exchanges or wallet providers
  • Phony websites with slightly misspelled URLs
  • Social media scams promising free Bitcoin or investment returns
  • Direct messages from "support" representatives

Always verify URLs before entering login credentials. Bookmark your exchange's official website and only access it through that bookmark. Never share your passwords or private keys with anyone.

Keep Software Updated

Whether you're using a mobile wallet, desktop wallet, or hardware wallet, keep all software current. Updates often include critical security patches that protect against newly discovered vulnerabilities.

Backup Your Wallet

If you use a software or hardware wallet, create a secure backup of your recovery phrase (seed phrase). Store this backup in a physically separate location—ideally in a safe or bank deposit box. Without your recovery phrase, you lose access to your Bitcoin permanently if your wallet is lost or damaged.
