Why You Need a Hardware Wallet

Before diving into the comparison, let's address why hardware wallets matter. When you store cryptocurrency on an exchange like Binance, Kraken, or Coinbase, you don't control your private keys—the exchange does. This creates significant risks:

  • Exchange hacks: History shows exchanges can be compromised, resulting in billions in losses
  • Insolvency: Exchange bankruptcies can freeze your assets indefinitely
  • Account freezes: Exchanges can lock accounts without explanation
  • Regulatory seizures: Government actions can block access to exchange-held funds

A hardware wallet stores your private keys on a dedicated device that never exposes them to the internet. When you want to send cryptocurrency, the device signs the transaction internally and only transmits the signed result. Your private keys remain completely isolated from potential threats.

Both Trezor and Ledger have established strong reputations in this space. Trezor, developed by SatoshiLabs in the Czech Republic, launched the world's first hardware wallet in 2014. Ledger, a French company, has become equally prominent with its line of secure devices. Both companies have served millions of customers worldwide.

Trezor Safe 3: Complete Overview

The Trezor Safe 3 represents Trezor's modern approach to accessible security. Released in October 2023, it succeeds the popular Trezor Model One while introducing significant security improvements at the same price point.

Security Architecture

The standout feature of Trezor Safe 3 is its dedicated Secure Element chip—an upgrade from the general-purpose microcontroller used in the Model One. This EAL6+ certified chip provides hardware-level protection against physical attacks. Even if someone steals your device and attempts to extract your keys using sophisticated tools, they cannot access your private keys.

Trezor has always championed open-source software, and the Safe 3 continues this tradition. Every aspect of the firmware is publicly available on GitHub for anyone to review, audit, and verify. Security researchers worldwide examine the code continuously, identifying potential vulnerabilities and confirming no backdoors exist. This transparency aligns perfectly with Bitcoin's core philosophy of "don't trust, verify."

The device introduces Shamir Backup to entry-level hardware wallets—a feature previously available only on premium models. Instead of relying on a single 12-24 word recovery phrase, you can split your backup into multiple shares (up to 16 total). You define how many shares are needed to recover your wallet, typically 3 of 5 or 5 of 7. This provides powerful redundancy:

  • Store shares in different locations
  • Distribute shares to trusted family members for inheritance planning
  • Recover your wallet even if one or two shares are lost

Ledger Nano S Plus: Complete Overview

The Ledger Nano S Plus launched in April 2022 as an upgraded version of the popular Nano S. It addresses the storage limitations that frustrated users of the original device while maintaining the compact form factor that made the Nano series popular.

Security Architecture

Ledger takes a different approach to security. The Nano S Plus features a Secure Element chip with CC EAL6+ certification—the highest security certification available for consumer hardware wallets. This chip isolates your private keys in a secure environment that signs transactions without ever exposing your keys to the main processor.

The device runs Ledger's proprietary operating system, BOLOS (Blockchain Open Ledger Operating System). This allows you to install multiple cryptocurrency apps simultaneously, each running in its own isolated environment. If one app contains a vulnerability, it cannot affect other apps or expose your keys.

Unlike Trezor's fully open-source approach, Ledger keeps some firmware components proprietary. This has sparked ongoing debates in the cryptocurrency community about transparency versus security through obscurity. Proponents argue that proprietary code makes it harder for attackers to study vulnerabilities. Critics counter that open-source allows independent verification of security claims. Both perspectives have merit, and both companies have maintained strong security records.

Ledger Nano S Plus features a brushed stainless steel cover with plastic backing, giving it a more premium feel than the Trezor Safe 3. The 128×64 pixel LED screen is slightly smaller in practice than Trezor's OLED, and some users find the display harder to read in bright light.

The device weighs 21 grams and measures 62.4×17.4×8.2mm. Its narrow profile some users find more pocket-friendly, though the difference is minimal. USB-C connectivity matches modern standards, but like Trezor Safe 3, there's no Bluetooth or mobile connectivity.

Key Differences Explained

Open Source Philosophy

The most fundamental difference between these wallets is their approach to transparency. Trezor Safe 3 is completely open source—every line of firmware code is published on GitHub for public review. Security researchers can audit the code, verify security claims, and identify potential vulnerabilities. This transparency aligns with cryptocurrency's ethos of trust minimization.

Ledger takes a hybrid approach. While they publish some code, the core BOLOS operating system remains proprietary. Ledger argues this makes it harder for attackers to find vulnerabilities, while critics note you must trust Ledger's security claims without independent verification.

For European users familiar with GDPR and privacy regulations, Trezor's transparency may provide additional peace of mind. However, both companies have maintained strong security records, and no widespread firmware vulnerabilities have been exploited in either device.

Shamir Backup: A Trezor Exclusive

Trezor Safe 3 includes Shamir Backup, a feature unavailable on Ledger Nano S Plus. This allows you to split your recovery seed into multiple shares distributed across different locations. For example:

  • Create 5 shares, require any 3 to recover
  • Store shares with different family members
  • Place shares in different geographic locations

If one share is lost or stolen, your funds remain secure—you need multiple shares to recover. This provides significant advantages for inheritance planning and disaster recovery.

Ledger Nano S Plus uses the standard BIP39 recovery phrase—a single 12-24 word phrase that must be protected completely. If lost, you cannot recover your funds. If stolen, your funds are compromised. While you can manually implement similar protection using third-party tools, it's not built into the device.

Software Experience

Ledger Live offers more integrated features out of the box. You can buy cryptocurrency directly through the app, swap tokens, stake supported assets, and manage NFTs—all without external services. This convenience appeals to users who want an all-in-one solution.

Trezor Suite focuses on core wallet functionality. While you can connect to third-party wallets for DeFi and advanced features, the base experience is simpler. For users who value privacy and transparency, this separation may be preferable. You can choose which third-party services to trust rather than relying on Ledger's integrated partners.

Setup Best Practices

Regardless of which device you choose, follow these security best practices:

  1. Purchase directly from the manufacturer. Never buy hardware wallets from eBay, Amazon, or third-party sellers. Compromised devices have been sold in the past.
  2. Verify tamper-evident seals. Both Trezor and Ledger use security seals. If they appear damaged or missing, contact customer support immediately.
  3. Write your recovery seed offline. Never type your seed phrase into a computer or phone. Use the provided recovery cards.
  4. Store your seed securely. Consider metal backup solutions that resist fire and water damage.
  5. Test recovery before storing significant funds. Practice recovering your wallet to ensure you recorded everything correctly.
  6. Enable PIN protection. Choose a memorable PIN that's not easily guessed.
