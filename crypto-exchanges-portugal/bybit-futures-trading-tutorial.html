What is Futures Trading?

Futures are financial contracts that obligate the buyer to purchase (or the seller to sell) an asset at a predetermined future date and price. In cryptocurrency futures trading, you're speculating on the future price of a digital asset like Bitcoin or Ethereum.

Unlike spot trading, where you buy and own the actual cryptocurrency, futures trading allows you to:

  • Go long: Profit when prices rise
  • Go short: Profit when prices fall
  • Use leverage: Control larger positions with less capital

Perpetual Futures vs. Traditional Futures

Bybit primarily offers perpetual futures contracts, which differ from traditional futures in a crucial way: they have no expiration date. You can hold a position indefinitely (as long as you maintain sufficient margin).

Traditional futures contracts expire on a specific date, requiring you to close or roll over your position. Perpetual contracts simplify trading by eliminating this complexity.

USDT-Margined vs. Coin-Margined Futures

Bybit offers two types of perpetual futures:

USDT-Margined (USDT Perpetual): Uses USDT as collateral. Your P&L is calculated in USDT, making it straightforward to track profits and losses. This is the recommended choice for beginners.

Coin-Margined (Inverse Perpetual): Uses the underlying cryptocurrency (e.g., BTC, ETH) as collateral. P&L is calculated in the base currency. This is more complex but can be tax-efficient for long-term holders.

Key Concepts You Must Understand

Before placing your first trade, understand these fundamental concepts:

Leverage

Leverage allows you to control a position larger than your initial margin. For example, with 10x leverage, a $100 margin controls a $1,000 position.

Example: If Bitcoin is at $50,000 and you open a $1,000 long position with 10x leverage:

  • Position size: $10,000
  • If BTC rises 5%, you profit $500 (50% ROI on margin)
  • If BTC falls 5%, you lose $500 (50% loss on margin)

Warning: Leverage magnifies both gains AND losses. High leverage is the #1 reason beginners blow up their accounts.

Margin

Margin is the collateral you deposit to open a leveraged position. Bybit offers two margin modes:

Isolated Margin: The margin is isolated to a single position. If liquidated, you only lose the margin allocated to that position. Recommended for beginners.

Cross Margin: Your entire account balance serves as margin for all positions. A liquidation can wipe your entire account. Use only when you understand the risks fully.

Liquidation

If your position moves against you and your margin falls below the maintenance margin requirement, Bybit forcibly closes (liquidates) your position to prevent further losses. You lose your entire margin.

Example: With 100x leverage, a 1% adverse move triggers liquidation. This is why responsible traders use stop-losses and modest leverage.

Funding Rates

Perpetual contracts use funding rates to keep contract prices close to spot prices. Funding is exchanged between long and short positions every 8 hours.

  • Positive funding: Longs pay shorts (market is bullish)

Check the funding rate before opening positions—holding during funding can add or subtract from your P&L.

Risk Management: The Most Important Section

Read this section multiple times. Risk management is the difference between long-term success and losing everything.

The Reality of Futures Trading

Futures trading is NOT a get-rich-quick scheme. Statistics show that 70-90% of retail traders lose money trading futures. Understanding and respecting risk is non-negotiable.

Position Sizing Rules

Never risk more than 1-2% of your account on a single trade. If you have $1,000, your maximum risk per trade is $10-$20.

Calculate position size:

  • Account size: $1,000
  • Risk per trade: 2% = $20
  • Stop loss distance: 3% from entry
  • Position size: $20 ÷ 0.03 = $666 maximum position

Always Use Stop-Losses

Never enter a trade without a stop-loss. A stop-loss automatically closes your position at a predetermined price, limiting losses.

Stop-loss placement strategies:

  • Below recent support levels (for longs)
  • Above recent resistance levels (for shorts)
  • Based on volatility (Average True Range)
  • Percentage-based (2-5% from entry for beginners)

Leverage Guidelines

Recommended leverage for experience levels:

  • Beginners (0-6 months): 2x-5x maximum
  • Intermediate (6-18 months): 5x-10x maximum
  • Advanced (18+ months): 10x-20x maximum
  • Professional: Rarely exceeds 20x

Myth busting: Professional traders don't use 100x leverage. That's a fast track to liquidation. They prioritize capital preservation over quick gains.

Risk-to-Reward Ratio

Only take trades where potential profit exceeds potential risk. A minimum 1:2 risk-to-reward ratio means:

  • Risking $100 to potentially make $200
  • Even with a 40% win rate, you're profitable

Diversification

Don't put all your margin in one position. Spread risk across:

  • Multiple trades (not simultaneously correlated)
  • Different assets
  • Different time horizons

Emotional Discipline

Trading psychology separates winners from losers. Follow these rules:

  • Never revenge trade: Accept losses and move on
  • Don't over-leverage after wins: Keep position sizing consistent
  • Use a trading journal: Document every trade, reasons, and outcome
  • Take breaks: Step away after significant wins or losses

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Over-Leveraging

Using 50x or 100x leverage turns trading into gambling. A 1-2% move against you triggers liquidation. Start with 3x-5x and increase only when consistently profitable.

2. No Stop-Loss

Markets can gap and move against you rapidly. Without a stop-loss, a small loss becomes catastrophic. Always set stops before entering.

3. Revenge Trading

After a loss, the urge to "make it back" leads to oversized positions and reckless decisions. Take a break, analyze what went wrong, then return with a clear mind.

4. Holding Through Funding

If funding is 0.01% every 8 hours, that's 0.03% daily or ~11% annually. Holding long positions with positive funding eats into profits. Check funding rates before entering.

5. Not Understanding Liquidation Price

Know your liquidation price before opening any position. Use Bybit's position calculator to see where you'll be liquidated based on leverage and margin.
