Understanding Spot Trading Fees: Maker vs Taker

Before diving into specific exchanges, let's clarify the terminology you'll encounter when comparing fees. Every trade on a crypto exchange involves two parties: someone creating liquidity (the maker) and someone taking it (the taker).

What Is a Maker Fee?

The maker is the trader who places a limit order that doesn't execute immediately. When you set a buy order at €50,000 for Bitcoin while the current price is €52,000, your order sits in the order book waiting to be filled. You're "making" a market by adding liquidity. Exchanges reward this behavior with lower fees because it improves market depth.

What Is a Taker Fee?

The taker is the trader who places an order that executes immediately, removing liquidity from the order book. Market orders always result in taker fees. If you click "Buy Now" or place a limit order that fills instantly, you're taking liquidity, which typically costs more.

Key insight: For most beginner and intermediate traders using market orders, taker fees are what you'll pay most often. Advanced traders who use limit orders strategically can benefit from lower maker fees.

How to Minimize Your Trading Fees

1. Use Limit Orders Instead of Market Orders

By placing limit orders that don't execute immediately, you qualify for maker fees, which are typically lower than taker fees. This strategy requires patience but can save 0.01-0.15% per trade.

2. Increase Your Trading Volume for VIP Status

If you're an active trader, concentrate your trading on one exchange to reach VIP tiers faster. Spreading volume across multiple exchanges means missing out on volume discounts.

3. Take Advantage of Native Token Discounts

On Binance, using BNB for fee payment saves 25%. While this requires managing BNB exposure, the savings compound significantly for active traders.

4. Compare Fees Before Each Major Trade

Exchange fee structures change. Before executing large trades, quickly check the current fee schedule on your preferred exchange.

5. Avoid Credit Card Deposits

Card deposits typically cost 1.8-4%, negating any fee savings from trading. Use SEPA bank transfers (free on most exchanges) for EUR deposits.
